How this campaign is played (rules frame). Mechanically this is treated as one Kill Team force, co-managed by three players — one player per fireteam (Sisters / Scavengers / Navy). The opposition and all NPCs are run by the Game Master (Peter). Progression and the cross-team economy run on Kill Team 2024 "Spec Ops" plus this campaign's own tracks. This codex gathers the narrative, names, places, role flavour, and objectives of the campaign.

The premise in one paragraph

Three Imperial warbands — a Sororitas mission, a clade of rogue tech-salvors, and a squad of Navy armsmen with a secret — are trapped together as the desert city-world of Sarrenhold falls to an Ork Waaagh!. To live, they must fight free together. To get anywhere, they must seize a warp-capable ship. And once free, each owes the others a debt — so they swear the Covenant of Dust, an oath to see every one of their separate errands through before they part. Fate has other plans: the Blackstone Fortress drags their ship from the warp, and the covenant is tested one final time in the ancient dark.

The three teams (the player force)

1. The Order of the Ember Veil — Adepta Sororitas

  • Models: Sisters of Battle figures.
  • Who they are: A small Sororitas mission attached to the Shrine of Saint Auria in Sarrenhold's cathedral district. Pious, disciplined, unbending.
  • Public goal: Recover the relics of Saint Auria (her ashes and gauntlet) from the desecrated shrine and bear them to the temple-world of Hallowmere for re-consecration.
  • Secret / tension: The least secretive team — but the most dangerous to the others, because their zeal makes them the ones who would punish the Navy's desertion and the Scavengers' theft if either were exposed.
  • Notable characters:
  • Sister Superior Castellan Helewys Korren — team leader; resolute, suspicious of the other two warbands but bound by necessity.
  • Hospitaller Sister Verene — the Medic role (see Roles). Also the only one who can open Saint Auria's reliquary-vault.
  • Imagifier Sister Davant — bears the Order's banner; the Zealot role.

2. The Station Forgers — Adeptus Mechanicus proxy (rogue salvage clade)

  • Models: "Station Forgers" scavenger miniatures.
  • Who they are: A semi-legitimate clade of void-scavengers and disavowed tech-priests who strip hulks and dead worlds for lost technology. They wear the Cog but no forge world owns them.
  • Public goal: Recover a data-vault buried in Sarrenhold's Administratum bunkers that holds the coordinates to a lost STC cache; then reach that world — Mygos Tertius — and lift the technology.
  • Secret / tension: They are not true Imperial representatives. In the bunker (Ch.1) they steal from a still-defended Imperial stronghold, and Adept Vire quietly pockets more than the coordinates — a fragment whose nature even the other Forgers don't fully know.
  • Notable characters:
  • Magos-Salvor Tobek — clade leader; cold, transactional, keeps the covenant because the maths favour it.
  • Adept Vire — the Tech-Adept role; interfaces with ancient, Ork, and Blackstone systems. A small, Jawa-like hooded scavenger; carries the secret fragment.
  • The Drudge — a lean, mind-wiped salvage-hauler on skittering arachnid legs, buried under lifter-servos and load-rigs; the Hauler role (needed to move heavy salvage and the STC core).

3. The Penitents of the Lance — Imperial Navy armsmen (deserters)

  • Models: Imperial Navy tactical / Breacher figures.
  • Who they are: A breacher squad from the frigate Lance of Tagaris. They fled their ship. To the galaxy they are deserters; in truth they were framed — blamed for a massacre ordered by their own commanding officer, Flag-Commodore Auger Drask, to bury his crime.
  • Public goal (the cover story): "We're just trying to get off-world like everyone else."
  • Secret / tension: Desertion. If the Sisters learn the truth before the Penitents can prove their innocence, the covenant could turn to bloodshed. Their real meta-goal: reach Vigil Station Antias and recover the sealed, authenticated fleet log the Lance of Tagaris mirrored there before Drask doctored his copy — the one record a tribunal can't dismiss as a fugitive's forgery. (Sabine Orr carries only a copy; alone it proves nothing.)
  • Notable characters:
  • Armsman-Sergeant Corvin Roe — team leader; carries the guilt of survival.
  • Flight-Officer Dax Kell — the Pilot role; the only hand who can fly the shuttle and, later, wrestle the captured kroozer through the warp.
  • Master-at-Arms Sabine Orr — the Strategist role; keeps the squad's hidden charts and a memorised map of old Imperial transit routes and the conspiracy that damns Drask.

Chronicle

Histories of the Covenant

How each warband came to be — and the Waaagh that threw three strangers into the same burning street.

Histories of the Covenant

Before the oath, before the Blackstone Fortress, before any of them learned to trust a stranger's back in a firefight — there were three warbands who had never meant to meet, and a dying world that forced them into the same burning street. These are their histories: how each came to be what it is, and the catastrophe that bound them together.

The Fall of Sarrenhold

Sarrenhold was never a jewel of the Imperium. It was a working world — a chalk-and-dust city-planet of the Karran Reach, its pale hab-spires raised over older bones, its people pious, poor, and patient. For nine centuries it paid its tithes in promethium and prayer and asked the wider galaxy for nothing but to be left alone. The wider galaxy, in the end, did not oblige.

The first sign was a star that should not have moved. Then the astropaths began to scream in their sleep, and the void-shipping out of the Reach simply stopped coming back. By the time the Imperial Navy picket at the system edge understood what was bearing down on them, Waaagh! Gutrakka was already in the outer dark — a migratory tide of greenskins ten thousand hulls strong, drawn to Sarrenhold the way flies are drawn to a wound, by nothing more complicated than the promise of a good fight. At its head rode Warboss Gutrakka Skulltoof, a slab of muscle and scrap-iron who had broken three Imperial worlds already and wore a necklace of their defenders' rank-pins to prove it.

The picket died in a single afternoon. The orbital defences lasted two days. Then the roks began to fall — burning mountains of welded iron that cratered the hab-districts and vomited out roaring mobs into streets that had known no war in living memory. The Planetary Defence Force fought, and was butchered. The Administratum locked its bunkers and burned its records. The shrine-bells rang the call to arms until the towers that held them came down. Within a week the city was a maze of fire and falling masonry, and the only question left to anyone still breathing was not how do we win but how do we get off this world before it dies under us.

There was one answer. Across the burning city, on a cracked spaceport pad in the southwest, sat a single airworthy shuttle — fuelled, sound, and utterly insufficient to save more than a handful. But it had a dead starter-drive that no Naval hand could wake, and the orks, with the low cunning of their kind, had raised a flak-tower over the tenements that could swat any craft out of the sky the moment it tried to climb. So the pieces lay scattered across a dying city: the Navy could fly the shuttle but not wake it; the scavengers could wake any dead machine but had no pilot and no claim; and a flak-tower stood over all of it that no single warband could break. To reach the shuttle was hard. To make it lift was harder. To do any of it alone was impossible.

The sailors saw the arithmetic first. Days before the city fell, the Navy squad had quietly found the Mechanicus scavengers and struck a bargain — you wake our engine, we fly you off this rock — and told no one. The warrior-nuns were drawn in last and from the outside, by a chance run-in and an open evacuation beacon, never knowing that the two factions they were about to fight beside were already bound to one another by a secret deal.

So it was that three warbands who had spent years on Sarrenhold without once exchanging a word — a mission of warrior-nuns, a clade of scavenger-priests, and a squad of armed sailors with a secret — found themselves running down the same collapsing avenue, toward the same impossible shuttle, under the same green tide. None of them trusted the others. All of them needed the others. The Waaagh did not care which of them lived. It only made certain that none of them could live apart.

The Penitents of the Lance

There were once forty hands aboard the frigate Lance of Tagaris who answered to Armsman-Sergeant Corvin Roe. Now there are a few. The rest are dead, or worse — listed in Imperial Navy rolls under a single black word: mutineer. It is a lie. It is the most important lie in their lives, and the reason they cannot stop running.

The truth is uglier and more ordinary than treason. Their commanding officer, Flag-Commodore Auger Drask, gave an order that should never have been given — a massacre, a civilian transport gutted to cover a smuggling arrangement that would have ended his career and his life had it come to light. Roe's squad were the witnesses. So Drask did what frightened, powerful men do: he buried the crime under their bodies, logged them as the perpetrators of a mutiny that never happened, and sent loyal Naval security to erase the only people who could contradict the record. The squad did the one thing left to them. They ran. They took a shuttle, jumped ship in the chaos of a warp-transit, and vanished into the underhives of the Karran Reach as deserters with a price on their heads and the truth locked in the memory of Master-at-Arms Sabine Orr, who had copied the real fleet logs before they fled. But a copy in a fugitive's hand proves nothing against a Flag-Commodore's doctored official record — it only tells them the truth and points the way. The thing that can actually prove the forgery is the untouched original the Lance of Tagaris auto-mirrored to a distant Naval relay hours before Drask rewrote his copy: sealed, time-stamped, and impossible to delete without the deletion itself being logged. That is the trap Drask is caught in. He cannot scrub the record without confessing — a Flag-Commodore who purges his own ship's mirror off a remote station is a Flag-Commodore the Adeptus begin to investigate. So for two years he has played the only safe game there is: leave the record buried in obscurity, where no one has cause to pull it, and hunt down instead the only people who know to point an investigator at it — Roe's squad. A proof no one knows to look for is no proof at all.

They have been hiding on Sarrenhold for two years, trading their considerable skills for anonymity, waiting for a chance to reach Vigil Station Antias, where that sealed original still sits in a data-core — the one record that, laid beside Sabine's copy, damns Drask. He knows it is the single thing he could not safely erase, and so he keeps a quiet watchman over it: a tripwire whose only task is to destroy that record the day anyone finally comes for it. Reaching Antias has always meant reaching it faster than his agent can burn it. Flight-Officer Dax Kell keeps a flyer ready at all times. They do not pray, exactly, but they keep their armour clean and their insignia stripped, and they do not let anyone — least of all a Battle-Sister of the Emperor — look too long at their faces.

Then the orks came, and the underhive was no longer a place to hide but a place to die. The Penitents needed off Sarrenhold as badly as anyone — more, perhaps, because for them capture by loyal Imperials would be as fatal as the greenskins. And the only road off the world ran through a shuttle they could not reach alone, beside a warrior-nun who would execute them on the spot if she ever learned what they truly were. So they did what they have become very good at doing. They kept their secret, they offered their guns, and they swore — to a Sister of the Emperor, of all people — that they were simply survivors, like everyone else, trying to live one more day.

It was even, mostly, true.

The Station Forgers

They will tell you, if you ask, that they are a salvage-clade of the Adeptus Mechanicus, and they will show you the Cog stamped on their pauldrons — scratched, looted, half-painted-over — to prove it. Both things are true. Neither is the whole truth. The Station Forgers are tech-scavengers — a clade that strips dead hulks, fallen satellites, and forgotten installations for the lost technology the Mechanicus prizes above all things, and which delivers its finds to forge worlds that are very careful never to ask too closely how they were got. No forge world owns the Forgers. No forge world quite disowns them either. They exist in the grey margin where the Imperium's hunger for archeotech outweighs its scruples about who does the digging.

Magos-Salvor Tobek leads them by the only authority a clade like this respects: he is the one whose calculations have kept them alive and paid. Around him orbit the others — Adept Vire, who can talk to dead machines as easily as living ones — and stands barely half the height of a grown man, a hooded scrap of a thing with lamp-lit eyes in the dark of its cowl; the Drudge, a mind-wiped salvage-hauler, lean as a wire skeleton but buried under decades of bolted-on lifter-servos and hauling-rigs and skittering on patient arachnid legs; and a rough handful of gun-helots and scrappers who were not born to the Cult Mechanicus so much as recruited from the wreckage it leaves behind. To an outsider they barely look human: lean frames lost inside ragged hoods and riveted rebreather masks, every one of them more salvaged iron than flesh — augmetic limbs, mechadendrites, and scavenged servo-arms grafted on wherever the work demanded it, until the line between the scavenger and the scrap they carry has all but worn away. They came to Sarrenhold chasing a rumour: that somewhere in the planet's Administratum archives lay a data-vault with the coordinates to a true Standard Template Construct cache — the kind of find that turns a disavowed salvage-clade into a clade that forge worlds compete to host.

They had located the vault, and begun the patient, deniable work of getting at it, when the sky fell. The Waaagh did not merely threaten their lives; it threatened their prize — and Tobek's cold arithmetic delivered an unwelcome answer. The data-vault sat in a hardened bunker the scavengers could not crack and carry under a planetary invasion, not alone, not in time. To get the coordinates off Sarrenhold, the Forgers would need muscle they did not have, and a way off-world they could not secure by themselves. The numbers, for once, demanded something other than self-interest. The numbers demanded allies.

The Order of the Ember Veil

The Order of the Ember Veil is a small thing as Sororitas orders go — a single mission, not a convent proper, founded three centuries ago to keep the Shrine of Saint Auria in Sarrenhold's cathedral district. Auria was a local saint, a hospitaller of the Adepta Sororitas who, the records say, walked into a plague-quarter that even the Ecclesiarchy had written off and did not walk out, but left it clean. Her ashes and her scorched gauntlet were interred beneath the cathedral, and the Ember Veil was raised to tend them: a handful of Battle-Sisters, never more than a dozen, sworn to the flame of her memory.

They are what such isolation makes of the faithful — harder than a great convent's Sisters, and narrower. With no cardinal-world's politics to soften them and no sister-house to temper their zeal, the Ember Veil distilled the Creed down to its iron core: the Emperor protects those who do not falter. Sister Superior Castellan Helewys Korren has led them for forty years, and in that time she has buried more of her Sisters than she commands, each loss tempering her a degree colder. Under her serve the Hospitaller Sister Verene, who carries Auria's own discipline of mercy, and the Imagifier Sister Davant, whose banner has flown over every grim little war the Order has fought in the Reach.

When the Waaagh came, the Order was not even at the shrine. Days before the invasion they had been sent down into the lower hab-sinks to purge a doomsday cult, and so the cathedral fell without them — which is why, when the campaign opens, the Sisters are fighting their way back into an Ork-held nave to recover what they were sworn to keep. Auria's relics could not be left to the greenskins. Helewys had already received, by astropath, the last command her distant superiors would ever send: the saint must not fall to the xenos. Bear her to Hallowmere, and let her rest among her sisters. It was, in effect, an order to abandon the world they had guarded for three hundred years — and to survive long enough to do it, the Ember Veil would have to do something no one in the Order had done in living memory. They would have to fight beside strangers they had every reason to despise.

The Order to Fire — A Tale of the Penitents of the Lance

Armsman-Sergeant Corvin Roe watched Flag-Commodore Auger Drask murder an innocent man eleven days before he was ordered to murder four thousand — and afterward he understood that the second order had been written into the first.

The innocent man's name was Voidsman Pell. He was nineteen, a deck-rating off the cargo tiers, and his one fatal error had been thoroughness: he had noticed that certain crates logged as Munitorum relief-grain massed like no grain ever loaded, and that they left the Lance of Tagaris in the dead of ship-night for hulls that filed no manifests. He took it up the chain, the way the schola had taught him, certain that was simply what an honest man did.

Drask had the boy brought before the assembled watch on the muster-deck, stripped of his rank-pins, named a thief in front of three hundred of his crewmates, and shot through the back of the skull while he was still trying to explain himself. Pilferage from the Emperor's stores, the charge sheet read. Sentence carried before the watch, that discipline be seen. Drask made them all stand and watch a child die for a crime he had not committed, and call it justice — and Roe stood in the front rank at rigid attention and said nothing, because that was what an armsman did.

He had hated himself for that silence with a cold, level hatred every day since. He had also learned the thing the silence taught: that men are not shot on the muster-deck for theft. They are shot for finding things. Whatever Pell had turned up in those manifests, Drask had killed a boy before three hundred witnesses to keep it buried — which told Roe precisely how far the Flag-Commodore would go, long before the night he was made to find out.

The order came at 03:14 ship-night, eleven days later, and it came to Roe's own gun-deck.

Master-at-Arms Sabine Orr took it off the vox-relay first. Sabine did not rattle; Roe had watched her hold level through boarding actions and a warp-incursion that had to be ended with flamers. So when she turned from the relay with her face gone the grey of cold ash, the dread was in his gut before she spoke. "Sergeant. The Commodore. You need to hear this one yourself."

The word, when Roe pressed the bud to his ear, was fire.

"Confirm the target." Because there had to be a mistake.

"Target confirmed." Drask's voice was smooth as poured oil. "Civilian transport Mercy of Kasr. Bulk hauler, refugee manifest, four thousand aboard. Weapons free. Reduce her, Sergeant, and account every round."

It was Sabine who understood it, fast, the way she understood everything. She had already dragged the transport's registry onto the gun-deck cogitator, and Roe watched her read it and go very still. "Sergeant," she said, low, for him alone, "that hull came out of the Veil sub-sector. Same lanes as the night-cargo. Those aren't war refugees — those are people who lived where the Commodore's crates came from." She looked up. "Whatever Pell found, they can say it out loud. The moment that ship makes a friendly dock, there are four thousand mouths that can hang Auger Drask. That is why she has to die. That is what we are being told to do."

"Sir," Roe said into the relay, slow and clear, "I will not fire on a refugee hull."

A silence. Then the oil was gone from Drask's voice, and what lay beneath it was worse than rage — the flat administrative calm of a man drawing a line through a name. "I had hoped your deck would be sensible. Stand by, Sergeant."

He did not argue. He did not threaten. He simply switched to the bridge channel — and Roe, frozen at the silent guns with his whole squad watching him, heard Flag-Commodore Drask give the same order to the forward batteries in the same poured-oil voice, and heard the Lance of Tagaris obey.

The deck heaved as the main lances fired. On the auspex the Mercy of Kasr — fat, slow, defenceless, four thousand souls — flared white, and broke, and went dark, in the time it took Roe to draw a single breath he never finished letting out. There had been no saving them. There had only ever been a choice about whose hand would do it, and Drask had simply found another hand. The refugees were dead either way, and Roe's refusal had bought them nothing at all — nothing but a gun-deck full of men who had just watched a Flag-Commodore of the Imperial Navy butcher four thousand civilians to keep a ledger clean.

Which made the gun-deck the next thing on the list.

The hatches sealed thirty seconds later, all at once, on Drask's authority. The vox died. And then his security came — loyal men, good men, armsmen no different from Roe's own, sent to put down what they had been told was a mutiny, because Drask had logged it that way before the Mercy of Kasr ever fired. He had written their guilt before they were guilty. That was the genius of him, and the horror.

They came in shooting and did not stop to ask, because they had been told there was nothing worth asking. Roe lost half his section in the first half-minute — hands he had trained, cut down at their own guns by Imperials who believed they were doing their duty. He saw Voidsman Ariq raise empty hands and be shot regardless. He saw the old deck-chief who had served Drask twenty years step between the guns and the killers, bellowing that these were loyal crew — and be shot for it too, because the only safe witness is a dead one, and Drask was spending lives now the way he spent rounds. Of forty hands on that deck, the night left five — and only because those five ran.

What came after, Sabine Orr would set down later in the stolen logs in her small exact hand, and she made it read as orderly, because that was how she survived things. It was not orderly. Halden blew a sealed bulkhead with a charge meant for boarding-work and took them into the maintenance crawls a step ahead of the kill-teams. Tace held a junction alone with the heavy stubber long enough for the rest to pass, then somehow fell back through them still breathing, white and shaking. And Sabine stopped at a records cogitator with loyalist boots already hammering the companionway and copied the true fleet log entire — Drask's order, the lie about Pell, the night-cargo manifests, all of it — because she understood, even running, that to flee without the proof was only a slower way to die a deserter.

And Dax Kell flew. Throne, how Kell flew — threw a stolen lighter off the rail through the Lance's own point-defence and a screen of pickets as though it were a thing of grace, laughing the whole way, because laughing was how Kell met the worst hours of his life. "They're logging us as mutineers!" he shouted over the engine-scream, hauling them into the warp-shadow where the frigate could not follow. "Forty good hands dead on that deck, and we're the traitors!"

No one answered. There was nothing to say. Behind them the Lance of Tagaris sailed on, spotless, her logs already rewritten, four thousand refugees and thirty-five of her own crew scrubbed into one tidy lie.

It was Sabine who named the hard part of it, hours later, when the shaking had stopped and someone had to say aloud what they had become. "This slate proves nothing," she said, flat and exact, turning the copied log over in her hands. "A record in a deserter's pocket against the word of a Flag-Commodore? Any tribunal in the Imperium takes Drask over five mutineers, and hangs us for forging it. He owns the official version now." She let that sit. "But the Lance mirrors her logs to the nearest Naval relay the instant they're written — standing order, automatic, every watch. She squirted the true log to Vigil Station Antias hours before Drask ever touched his copy. It's sealed there. It's stamped with the station's own seal, and the time on it predates his lie."

"Then he wipes it," said Halden. "He's a Flag-Commodore. He sends a tech-priest and it's gone."

"No." Sabine almost smiled, and there was nothing warm in it. "That's the beauty of the thing. You don't purge a sealed relay-log without the purge itself being logged — and a Flag-Commodore who scrubs the Lance's mirror off a distant station is a Flag-Commodore the Adeptus start asking questions about. Touching it is the confession. So he won't. He'll do the cleverer thing — leave it exactly where it sits, one routine data-dump among a thousand in a sleepy archive nobody will ever have cause to pull, and make certain no one is left alive who knows to go and pull it." She looked round at them, the only steady thing in the hold. "That's what we are. Not loose ends — the only mouths in the galaxy that can point an auditor at Vigil Antias. That is why he wants us dead and not merely gone. And it is why, on the day we finally move on that station, there will already be someone of his standing over that record, waiting to burn it the moment we reach for it — because it is the one thing he never dared burn himself." She turned the copied log over once more. "We run to Antias. We stay alive. And we put the two side by side in front of someone who matters — before his watchman can put a las-bolt through the only proof we'll ever get."

Roe sat in the shuddering dark of the lighter and made the only promise he had left. Not to the squad, who needed no convincing. Not to the Throne, which had not been listening on the muster-deck either. He made it to a boy named Pell, and to four thousand strangers whose names he would never learn, who had died so that one man's ledger might stay clean. Someone is going to read what is in these logs, he thought, if it costs me the rest of my life. And when they do, they are going to say his name aloud — the way it should have been said on that muster-deck.

It would take two years. It would take a hidden life in the underhives of a dusty world called Sarrenhold, insignia stripped and faces down, a flyer always fuelled and a road to Vigil Antias that the war kept never quite offering them. It would take, in the end, an alliance with a warrior-nun who would have executed all five of them where they stood, had she ever learned what the Imperial record said they were.

But that was all still ahead. For now there was only the warp-shadow, and five survivors of forty, and the proof locked in Sabine Orr's logs — and a sergeant keeping faith with the dead, the only congregation that had ever told him the truth.

The Dead Don't Bargain — A Tale of the Station Forgers

Magos-Salvor Tobek had a rule, and the rule was this: a corpse is worth exactly what you can carry out of it, minus what it costs you to go in.

He had built a life on that rule, and a clade to go with it, and the clade had kept him breathing long past the age when the Cult Mechanicus recalls an honest magos to a forge world to die useful. Tobek was not honest, which was the secret of his longevity. No forge world owned the Station Forgers; no forge world quite disowned them either. They were the hands the Mechanicus reached for when it wanted a thing pulled out of the dark and did not wish to be told how it had been got — welcomed when the salvage was good, unheard of when an inquisitor came asking. Tobek had stopped resenting the arrangement decades ago, and started pricing it. Everything had a price. That was the one article of faith the Forgers truly kept.

He recited the rule to himself now, watching the drop-feed from a helot's helm-pict as the man crept down a corridor that had not known light or air in nine hundred years. The derelict was an old Imperial system-runner, dead in the void above some nameless waystation, and somewhere in its frozen guts was a cogitator-core worth more than the lives of everyone aboard Tobek's salvage-barge combined. The maths were good. The maths were always why they came.

"Pressure door," the helot's voice crackled — a young one, a numbered conscript Tobek had not troubled to name, because the named ones cost more to lose. "Sealed. Old locking glyphs."

"Then we have reached the part," said Tobek, "where I earn my tithe-share. Vire."

Adept Vire was already moving — though moving flattered it, and there was so little of Vire to move. A small thing, barely half the height of a grown man, all but swallowed in a patched red cowl, two lamp-lit eyes the only proof that a face hid somewhere in the dark of the hood. It did not walk so much as drift, low and quick, a child's silhouette wrapped in a scavenger's rags. The other Forgers had long since stopped wondering what was under the hood. They only knew that doors opened for the little adept, that it was worth a dozen of them, and that there was something in the way it tilted its head at a dead machine — listening — that made even the Drudge give it room.

Vire laid one augmetic hand against the door. "It's lonely," it murmured, in the soft child's-voice that never stopped unsettling Quillon. "Nine hundred years. It just wants someone to talk to." Its fingers moved over the glyphs in a pattern that meant nothing to anyone living. The door shuddered, groaned, and ground open onto blackness. "There. Be kind to it on the way through. It remembers being a ship."

The young helot went through, and the others followed, and that was when the derelict killed him.

It was old Imperial paranoia — a tech-ward, a security construct left to sleep across the centuries, and it woke now with a shriek of dead servos and put a las-bolt through the conscript's throat before any of them had finished crossing the threshold. He went down gurgling, clawing at a wound no medi-kit would ever close. Reaver Quillon was past him in an instant — and Quillon, in motion, was a thing of horror and grace both: a feral salvage-rat the clade had broken to discipline, his torso riding high on a nest of skittering arachnid legs, industrial cutters screaming as they spun up. He took the construct apart in a shower of sparks with the open joy of a man doing the one thing he was built for. But it was already too late for the helot, and everyone in the corridor knew it.

Beside the dying boy, a second helot had dropped to one knee — an older one, scarred, carrying a looted carbine he had not been ordered to bring and a steadiness the young ones never had. He did not run. He put two rounds into a second construct unfolding out of the dark, calm as a man on a practice range, and then he stayed low at the dying conscript's side. Not because it would help. Because someone should.

Tobek logged that. He logged everything.

He looked at the dying boy for precisely as long as it took to calculate. The wound was mortal. Stabilising it would cost two operatives, a medi-kit, and the time it would take the construct's siblings to wake. The core was nineteen metres ahead. The arithmetic was ugly, and honest, and Tobek had built his whole life on never lying to himself about arithmetic.

"Leave him his sidearm," the Magos-Salvor said quietly. "Quillon, the Drudge — with me. We have nineteen metres and a fortune at the end of them. Vire, keep the dead things sleeping." His optic swung once to the older helot still kneeling in the dark. "You. Designation."

"Sarn." The man did not look up from the boy.

"Sarn." Tobek had already turned away. "You live, you keep the gun. Move."

The Drudge came when it was called, the way it always did. It was lean to the point of skeletal — there was hardly a man left inside it — but what the augmetics had taken in flesh they gave back in patient, tireless strength: a tall, near-mute hauler riding the same skittering arachnid frame as Quillon, kitted not for killing but for burden, an empty load-rack rising off its spine and lifter-servos folded at its flanks like the limbs of something waiting to be told what to lift. Where Quillon's optics burned, the Drudge's were dim — half-dead, the lamps of a mind long since scraped out and sold for parts. It did not look at the dying boy. Tobek was not certain it could.

And yet — he would swear to it later, against all sense — as the hauler stepped over the conscript, its great head turned, slow, and tracked the boy for the length of a single stride, as though far down beneath the wiped circuits and the bolted iron one last ember had stirred, looked, and gone cold again. A trick of the servos. It had to be. The Drudge did not grow a mind. The Forgers had not paid for one.

They took the core. Of course they took the core; the maths were good. Sarn knelt with the boy until the boy was finished, then rose, and kept the carbine, and was thereafter — to his own quiet astonishment — a man with a name on the roll instead of a number. The clade lost a conscript and gained a gunner, and on Tobek's ledger that balanced to a profit. He wrote it down as one, and did not let himself wonder why his hand was slow to do it.

It was only later, back aboard the barge with the cogitator humming in the hold, that Tobek noticed Adept Vire had taken something too.

The Magos-Salvor said nothing at first. He watched the little adept turn a small, dark object over and over in its augmetic fingers — a shard of something that drank the light rather than reflecting it, something that had been on no manifest, something Vire had slipped from the derelict's heart and shown to no one. He watched Vire smile at it the way it smiled at waking machines. And he watched the dim lamps of the Drudge, across the hold, swing toward the shard and hold there a moment too long before they dulled again.

"What is it," Tobek said. It was not quite a question.

"I don't know yet." Vire did not look up, and the child's-voice had gone soft and far away. "It's not lonely. That's the difference. The ship was lonely. This —" it lifted the shard, and for an instant the lamp-lit eyes beneath the hood caught a light the shard had no business casting — "this has been waiting. There's a difference between a thing that has been left somewhere, and a thing that has been kept."

A corpse is worth what you can carry out of it. Tobek filed the observation away, the way he filed everything, against the day the maths would need it. He did not yet know that the day would come on a chalk-dust world called Sarrenhold, in a hardened Administratum bunker, over a data-vault holding the coordinates to a true Standard Template Construct cache — the kind of find that turns a disavowed clade into one the forge worlds compete to host. He did not know that the same world held a far stranger shard than Vire's; nor a green tide ten thousand hulls strong already swinging toward it; nor that the only road off it would run through the dead starter-drive of a stranded shuttle and a bargain with armed strangers that his arithmetic, for the first time in a long and profitable life, would prove wholly unable to talk him out of.

He knew only that Vire was keeping a secret; that the Drudge had looked at a dying boy and at a waiting shard with the very same dead eyes; and that secrets, like corpses, eventually cost exactly what you can carry out of them.

The maths, just then, were still good.

They would not stay that way.

Ash and Iron — A Tale of the Order of the Ember Veil

Three days before the sky fell, the Order of the Ember Veil left their shrine to go and burn a heresy, and that was why they were not there when the orks came.

It had begun, as these things do, with whispers. In the deep hab-sinks of Sarrenhold's lower city — the sunless levels where the chalk-dust never settled and the Emperor's light came secondhand — a cult had bloomed almost overnight. They called themselves the Listeners, and they had begun, all of them at once, to hear the same thing: a vast slow grinding in the dark between the stars, a sound that the Order's own astropaths had been screaming about in their sleep for a week. The Listeners did not know it was a Waaagh. They only knew it was the end, and they had decided, in the way of the weak and the frightened, to fling open the door and welcome it.

The Cardinal's summons had reached the cathedral by nightfall. Purge them. Quietly. Before the panic spreads. And so Sister Superior Helewys Korren had taken her Order down into the dark, leaving Saint Auria sealed in her shrine behind a door three centuries old and a city's worth of faith — because the shrine had always been the safest place on Sarrenhold, and because it never once occurred to her that she might not be coming back to it.

They found the Listeners in a flooded sump-temple on the ninety-first level, and Sister Novice Cael killed her first human being there.

She had expected monsters. What she found were people — gaunt, grey, hollow-eyed people swaying together in the dark and chanting at the ceiling, and it was so much worse than monsters that for a moment Cael could not move. Then one of them came at her out of the press with a rusted blade and a beatific smile, and her body did what three years of training had taught it to do before her mind could object, and the man folded around her bolt-round and was simply gone. Cael stood over what she had done and shook.

"They are not innocent." Helewys had come to stand beside her, surveying the temple the way a surgeon surveys a tumour. "Understand that now, Novice, and you will save yourself a great deal of grief later. They were offered the Emperor's light and they chose the dark. Pity them if you must. But do not hesitate over them." She raised her voice without raising it at all, that flat grey command that the world obeyed. "Imagifier. The standard."

Sister Davant lifted the banner — the ember-and-ash standard that had flown over every grim little war the Order had ever fought — and at the sight of it the Listeners' chant turned to screaming, and the screaming turned to a charge. Sister Retributor Aldena met it without expression, her heavy flamer turning the front rank into a wall of fire, the light of it throwing all their shadows huge and writhing up the sump-temple walls. It was over in minutes. It usually was.

It was Verene who found the girl.

She could not have been more than fourteen, half-drowned in the sump-water and clutching a scrap of heretical scripture like a child's toy, and she was alive, and the rest of the Order would have walked past her without a second glance. Helewys certainly did. But Hospitaller Sister Verene knelt in the filth in her white surcoat and gathered the shivering girl up and pressed a wad of synth-skin to the gash in her scalp, murmuring the same thing she murmured to the dying and the terrified alike: "Breathe. You are not dying. The fear is a candle — let it burn, but do not let it spread."

"Sister." Helewys's voice carried a warning. "She is one of them."

"She is fourteen, Sister Superior, and she was lied to by men who are now ash." Verene did not look up. There was steel under the warmth, the way there had always been; it was why Auria's own discipline had passed to her and not to another. "The Saint walked into a plague-quarter that the Ecclesiarchy had written off, and she did not walk out, but she left it clean. I will not leave a child to drown because she was afraid of the dark. We can disagree about it later."

For a moment the two of them held there — the cold commander and the merciful one, the two halves of what the Order was — and it was Helewys who looked away first, as she always, in the end, did with Verene. "Bring her, then. The Cardinal can decide what she is." And that, Cael would think later, was the truest thing she ever learned about the Ember Veil: that Helewys would burn a world for duty, and that Verene was the only soul alive who could make her stop and carry something out of the fire.

They were climbing back toward the upper city, the rescued girl stumbling between them, when the sky broke.

It came as a sound first — not the grinding the Listeners had heard, but its arrival: a tearing roar as the first ork rok came down through the cloud, and then another, and another, burning mountains of iron falling across the city they had been bound to protect. The ground heaved. Far above and very far away, on the high spine of the cathedral district, Cael saw the spire of the Shrine of Saint Auria catch the light of a fire that had not been there an hour ago.

The Order stood in the broken street and watched their home begin to burn from the wrong side of a dying city.

"The Listeners were right," Cael whispered, before she could stop herself. "About the end."

"They were right about the sound." Helewys was already moving, already turning the Order toward that distant burning spire, her bolter in her hands and forty years of iron in her spine. "They were wrong about the door. You do not open it and kneel. You stand in it, and you make them come through you." She did not look back to see if they followed. She never had to. "Saint Auria is in that fire, Sisters. Three hundred years we have kept her. We are going to go and get her back, through every greenskin in this city if we must — and Throne help whatever stands between us and her."

They had crossed perhaps half the city — through streets that were no longer streets, past the burning and the dead and the orks who were always, somehow, between them and the cathedral — when the vox-bead in Helewys's gorget woke with two voices at once.

The first was their own astropath, far off at the convent-world, the signal frayed almost to nothing: ...the saint must not fall to the xenos... recover her... bear her off-world... The same command, over and over, the last order the Order would ever receive. Rescue Auria. Get her clear.

The second was closer, and stranger — a flat repeating tone under a man's clipped voice, a beacon broadcasting on an open Naval frequency. Evacuation point. Flightworthy craft, spaceport, southwest. Any Imperial forces able to reach it, converge. Repeat — there is a way off this world. A way off. A craft. The relic and the road, in a single signal.

They found the source of it eight streets later, in the lee of a toppled manufactorum: a knot of armed men in the void-armour of the Imperial Navy, insignia stripped — Helewys noted that, and filed it, and said nothing — holding a chokepoint against an ork mob with the cold economy of professionals. Their sergeant, a hard-faced man named Roe, lowered his weapon a careful fraction when he saw the Order's colours, and the bargain was struck in the brisk shorthand of people who have no time to be anything but honest.

"You're making for the cathedral," Roe said. It was not a question. "Your shrine. Whatever's in it."

"Saint Auria. And then off this world, by your beacon." Helewys's gaze went over him — the discipline, the stripped insignia, the wary eyes — and weighed him, and found him useful, which was as near to trust as she came. "You have a craft."

"We have a craft. We have a problem with it, too." Roe nodded south, to where a squat ork tower hunched over the tenements, its guns already swinging to track something in the smoke. "Flak tower. Nothing lifts off this city while it stands, and it's too much for my squad alone. It might be enough for yours and mine together."

So it was agreed, there in the ruin, between a Sister of the Emperor and a man with no insignia: the Order would cut their way to the cathedral and recover their saint; both warbands would converge on the tower and break it; and together they would reach the shuttle and leave Sarrenhold to the green tide.

What Helewys did not know — what she had no way to know, and what would have changed everything if she had — was that Sergeant Roe already had a second bargain folded in his pocket. That his flightworthy craft had a dead starter-drive no Naval hand could wake. That somewhere across the same burning city a clade of disavowed Mechanicus scavengers was already moving toward the same spaceport on the strength of a deal the Navy had struck days before — you wake our engine, we fly you off this rock — and that the "simple survivors" the Sister Superior had just sworn to bleed beside were keeping rather more than one secret apiece.

She would learn all of it, in time, and the learning would nearly undo them. For now there was only the bargain, and the beacon, and the burning spire — and the long road back to it, and a Sister Superior walking toward her overrun cathedral as though the whole green tide of the orks were merely a queue she intended to clear.

Teams & Character Progression

The roster of the Covenant — the three fireteams, their named operatives, and how each character grows across the ten-chapter arc. This document extends the canon in (which fixes the leads and the special roles) by completing each roster and adding a progression layer.

Rules frame: progression runs on Kill Team 2024 "Spec Ops" (XP → ranks → Battle Honours & specialisms, Battle Scars, Requisition, Stash). This doc owns the arcs: each character's start → turning point → end, and which named capstone they unlock. The "command stream" maps to lead specialisms, "specialisation" to support specialisms, and each capstone is a named Battle Honour gated to surviving that character's spotlight chapter.

Roster shape

Each team is built as one lead + two essential support (the campaign's core three, already canon) with an optional expansion to five for groups with the models and appetite for a bigger force. So each fireteam is 3 to 5 operatives; the whole Covenant is 9 to 15.

  • Core 3 (always present): the lead and the two operatives who hold the team's story-gated special roles — losing them is what forces a chapter's hard branch, so they anchor every roster.
  • Expansion to 5 (optional): two additional support operatives per team — the battlefield muscle (heavy weapons, breaching, melee) that rounds the fireteam into a self-sufficient unit and gives more bodies to spread progression across.

Deployment note (for Peter): with 9–15 operatives this is larger than a standard Kill Team. Suggested handling — not every operative fields every chapter. Each chapter spotlights specific operatives (see each chapter's Role Spotlight); field a chapter-appropriate subset and let the roster rotate. This keeps battles KT-sized and lets progression spread across the whole cast rather than pooling on six models.

How progression works (the framework)

A five-part model, kept rules-light for Peter to formalise:

  1. Personal arc — every operative has a start state → turning point (their spotlight chapter) → end state. Narrative growth, the spine of their progression.
  2. Advances — operatives earn improvements as the campaign runs. Two streams:
  • Leads advance toward command (better auras, more orders, force-wide buffs).
  • Support advance toward specialisation (deeper at their one job — sharper shooting, better breaching, harder melee).
  1. Battle scars — wounds that persist (ties to the Wounded roster state). The Hospitaller (Sister Verene) clears these between missions; without her, scars stick and shape a character (a limp, a cold rage, a crisis of faith) as much as they hinder.
  2. Renown & the Covenant — shared progression. Conduct in Chapters 5–7 moves the Suspicion / Covenant track; a true Covenant unlocks force-wide "spend the oath" efforts (Ch.9) and better endings. Cross-team trust is itself a progression axis.
  3. Signature capstone — each operative has one campaign-defining unlock, earned by surviving and completing their spotlight, that pays off at the climax (Ch.9) and the epilogue (Ch.10).

Progression milestones are chapter-tied — an operative's biggest growth lands when their spotlight chapter resolves, so advancement feels like the story rather than bookkeeping.

The Last City

escape the planet

As Sarrenhold falls to the Orks, three warbands who do not trust one another must cross a dying city, each seize what they came for, and reach the one shuttle that can still lift off — together, or not at all.

Where we are

This is the beginning. The three teams have lived on Sarrenhold for years; none of them has any grand design here beyond getting out alive — and carrying off the one thing each came to this world to find. The sky is full of Ork landing craft. The hive-city's defence has collapsed. A single flightworthy shuttle sits on a spaceport pad across the city — their one way off Sarrenhold — but two things stand between it and the sky. The first is an Ork anti-air tower the greenskins have raised in the ruins, which will blast any craft out of the air the moment it tries to lift off. The second is the shuttle itself: its starter-drive is dead, and no Naval hand can wake it. This is why the Navy and the Scavengers are already, quietly, working as one — a bargain struck before the Sisters ever arrive (you wake our engine, we fly you off this rock), and a secret the Sisters do not know they are about to fight alongside.

Nothing carries in (this is Chapter 1). Everything that happens here is written forward.

Briefing

The bells of Sarrenhold have stopped ringing. Where there were prayers there is now only the two-stroke cough of Ork engines and the screaming of a city learning it will not see another dawn. Chalk-dust hangs in the red light, thick enough to taste. Somewhere across the rooftops a shuttle waits on a cracked landing pad, fuelled and faithless — but over the tenements an Ork flak-tower rakes the sky, and nothing lifts off this world while it still stands. Bring it down, reach the shuttle, and live. You came here for different reasons. You will leave for the same one. Move.

The board

A four-quadrant tabletop representing a dense desert hive-district. Chalk-white hab-blocks, collapsed spires, narrow choked alleys, washing-lines and market awnings, wrecked Imperial vehicles. Board-wide pressure: a falling-city clock — escalating Ork waves each round and a sense that the district is collapsing behind them. Sightlines are short in the alleys, long across the two open spaces (cathedral approach and spaceport tarmac).

  • NW — Cathedral District. Tall spires, rubble-choked streets, the fortified nave of the Shrine of Saint Auria. Towering walls give cover; Ork shootas perch above. The Sisters' ground.
  • NE — Administratum Bunkers. Narrow alleys, locked blast doors, a hardened records vault. The Scavengers' ground — and not held by Orks (see Opposition).
  • SW — Spaceport. Open tarmac and landing pads, little cover, the one flightworthy shuttle. The Navy's ground; a killing field for anyone caught in the open.
  • SE — Residential Zone. Collapsed tenements, plenty of cover, and the Ork anti-air (flak) tower raised over a tenement square — the gun that holds the whole city's sky hostage. The joint objective.
  • Centre — Market Square. Where the teams begin scattered around the edges and where they must converge.

Objectives

Shared objective.

  1. Bring Down the Flak Tower. All three fireteams must converge on the SE residential zone and destroy the Ork anti-air tower together — it is too well defended for any one team. While it stands, the shuttle cannot lift off without being blown out of the sky.
  2. Reach the Shuttle. Once the tower falls and the sky is clear, every surviving character must make it to the SW spaceport pad and board before the Ork waves overrun it. The shuttle lifts once.
  3. Wake the Engine. The shuttle's starter-drive is dead — the Navy can fly her but cannot wake her. At the pad, under closing Ork waves, the Scavengers' tech-adept (Adept Vire) must bring the cold drive to life before Flight-Officer Kell can lift off. This is the Navy–Scavenger bargain made flesh: the two warbands are bound to each other here whether the Sisters know it or not.

Per-team objectives.

  • Sisters — Recover the Relics. Storm the Ork-held nave in the NW cathedral and retrieve the ashes and gauntlet of Saint Auria from the reliquary.
  • Scavengers — Crack the Vault. Infiltrate the NE Administratum bunker and extract the data-vault holding the STC coordinates. They also owe the Navy a debt at the spaceport: Adept Vire must wake the dead shuttle (see Wake the Engine).
  • Navy — Ready the Shuttle. Reach the SW spaceport, hold the pad, and get the shuttle fuelled and flight-ready — but her starter-drive is dead, and no Naval hand can wake it. That last spark is the Scavengers' to give; without it, Kell has nothing to fly.

The Collapse track (the timer). A track of 8. +1 at the end of each round; +1 whenever an informant escapes the board (see Twists). At each even step an Ork wave arrives; at 8 the spaceport is overrun — the shuttle lifts immediately with only the models in the SW pad zone (everyone else is left behind). Bring the tower down and reach the pad before it fills.

Hidden / optional.

  • Vire's Fragment (secret). In the bunker, Adept Vire may spend an action to pocket an unlogged archeotech fragment. Sets Vire's Fragment: Dormant (the late-campaign payoff begins). If any non-Forger model has line of sight when he takes it, +1 Suspicion.
  • Save the Faithful (optional). Escort a knot of civilian tokens to the pad. Each one extracted = +1 Salvage Point and counts toward the Civilians Saved flag (echoes at Ch.5 and Ch.10), but each escort action you spend lets the Collapse track tick — the cost is time.

Suspicion this chapter (it sets the campaign's starting level): +1 per Imperial holdout killed in the bunker (the others witness the Scavengers/Navy spill Imperial blood); +1 if Vire is seen taking the Fragment. Bluffing or subduing the holdouts costs nothing. Start the campaign at the total (minimum 0).

Loot: optional Salvage tokens in the market and bunker — +1 Salvage Point each carried to the shuttle (no Suspicion here; it is a dying city, not a shrine).

The Opposition

  • Waaagh! Gutrakka — the main threat. Ork mobs flood the cathedral approach, the residential zone, and the spaceport in escalating waves keyed to the falling-city clock. Shootas on the high walls; a Deff Dread or Killa Kan as the tower's anchor for the joint fight.
  • The bunker holdouts (NOT Orks). The NE Administratum bunker is held by a handful of desperate, exhausted Imperial defenders who have no idea who the Scavengers are or what they want. The Scavengers must sneak, bluff, or fight through fellow Imperials — a moral knot the other teams may notice and remember.
  • Civilians & double agents. Panicked locals stream through every quadrant. Some are harmless; some are informants who, if they see the wrong thing (the Navy's insignia stripped off, the Scavengers looting an Imperial vault), bolt to fetch Orks or holdout reinforcements. You don't know which is which until they run.

  • One shuttle, hard choices. When the Collapse track hits 8, only models in the SW pad zone get out. Civilians and wounded that slow the retreat may be the ones left behind.

Flight Through Fire

launch and escape orbit

One shuttle, one overworked pilot, and a sky full of Ork fighters between the teams and the warp-capable ship that is their only way out of the system.

Where we are

The shuttle is airborne with Sarrenhold burning beneath it. It cannot make the warp on its own — it must claw to high orbit and reach a faster-than-light vessel. Between here and there is a gauntlet of Ork flak, fighta-bommers, and the debris of a dead planet's defence grid.

This chapter runs in two acts. Act 1 — the Climb is always played: a fast, no-miniatures push-your-luck race to orbit resolved with tracks and a few d6. Act 2 — Dead Stick is a fully-statted skirmish aboard a derelict station, played only if the climb fails. A healthy pilot who flies clean skips Act 2 and starts Chapter 3 ahead; a wounded pilot bleeds for scraps on the station first. That gap is the point of the chapter.

Carried in from Ch.1:

  • Pilot status (Healthy / Wounded) — the single biggest input. Sets the Evade difficulty below.
  • Botched launch? — yes if Kell was wounded or if Adept Vire failed to wake the drive cleanly at the pad. A botched launch starts the climb at Integrity 4 instead of 6.
  • Wounded roster — Ch.1 casualties ride in the hold; Sister Verene may have stabilised them. They cannot act in Act 1, and are models-at-risk in Act 2.

Briefing

The hold stinks of promethium and fear-sweat. Through the viewport the curve of Sarrenhold falls away, and above it the void boils with green-painted hulls and tracer-fire. Flight-Officer Kell's hands are white on the yoke. "Strap in and shut up," he says, to gods and passengers alike. "This is the part where we find out how lucky we are."

The board

Two boards, one per act. Only set up the second if the climb fails.

Act 1 — the Climb needs no terrain: a cockpit card, an Integrity track of 8 boxes (6 if the launch was botched), an Altitude track (0 → 8 = orbit), and the Threat deck (9 cards, shuffled face-down, one revealed per round). It is played in the hold, on the table edge — pure nerve, no movement: a race to claw to orbit before the void chews the shuttle open.

Act 2 — Dead Stick plays out aboard Waystation HV-7 "Caltrop," a dead Imperial orbital fuel-depot the crippled shuttle limps to. A compact four-zone board, roughly 22"x30":

  • A. Dock — deployment zone and extraction point. If Kell was Wounded (no clean hard-dock), the Orks deploy here, with you, in the opposite corner — a far worse start.
  • B. Spine Corridor — long open killing-lane joining every zone. Light cover only.
  • C. Enginarium — holds 2 Fuel tokens. Hazard: a model that ends its activation here without a sealed door between it and the breach takes one mortal-equivalent hit on a 5+ (venting plasma).
  • D. Cargo Hold — holds 1 Parts token, behind a sealed door: Vire opens it for free; anyone else must spend an action to Breach (4+).

Objectives

Act 1 — claw to orbit. Reach Altitude 8 with Integrity ≥ 1. Each round a Threat is revealed (the deck is shuffled) and the table picks one shared response (Evade / Brace / Afterburner / Crew); full dice in The mini-game. Hit Altitude 8 → Dock Clean. Drop to Integrity 0 first → the climb fails and you play Act 2.

Act 2 — strip the Caltrop. Carry at least 2 of the 3 loot tokens (2x Fuel, 1x Parts) back to Zone A and get every surviving model into the deploy zone by the end of Round 4.

  • Full success: 3 tokens recovered and extracted.
  • Partial: exactly 1–2 tokens.
  • Failure: 0 tokens, or any model left behind when the clock runs out (lost, captured, or worse — GM's call).

Choose the game style (decide before deploying Act 2 — the campaign can switch freely between the two):

  • Kill Team mission — a full fireteam of up to 6 operatives drawn from any team aboard. A proper firefight. Use the enemy counts in the left column of The opposition.
  • Blackstone-style (3 characters) — only the three role-leads, one per team: Dax Kell (Pilot, las-pistol), Adept Vire (Tech-Adept, opens sealed doors), Sister Verene (Hospitaller, heals 1 Wound per activation). Tighter, tenser, survival-horror. Halve the enemy counts (right column).

The headline stakes (pilot online vs. not):

  • Kell healthy → flies clean. No wounds, teams rested, and you bank +1 Covenant: start Ch.3 holding the initiative on Turn 1 and reveal the kroozer-bridge objective for free.
  • Kell wounded → Dead Stick. You must play Act 2 for any reward at all; the crew loses time (Suspicion +1), Kell can't pilot the hard-dock (Orks deploy inside the dock), and salvage is the only upside on offer. Full rewards table in Outcomes.

Tracks. Integrity 8 (6 if botched). Altitude 0 → 8 (8 = orbit). Strain on Kell starts at 0. Afterburner: 1 charge for the whole climb. Reach Altitude 8 → Dock Clean. Integrity 0 first → crash → Act 2.

Not clean, never clean — a real challenge either way, and the difference between a rested pilot and a hurt one is the whole point.

Briefing

The board

A descending subterranean complex: collapsed entry galleries, a vast machine-hall, sealed vault doors, and the STC core chamber at the bottom. Darkness (limited visibility), dormant machinery that activates in stages, narrow extraction route back up (the climb out under load is its own ordeal).

Objectives

This mission is played in two acts on one board: a silent descent, then a running fight back up. Win when the STC core leaves the board via the Surface Breach. Lose if the breach seals with the core still inside, or the force is wiped.

Primary — take the core and get it out. Three steps:

  1. Reach the STC Core Chamber — quietly (Act 1).
  2. Claim it: Adept Vire spends an action to interface the vault. Opening the vault is claiming the core — one act (a Heretek Interface; see Suspicion), and it frees the core intact. No Vire → a model spends 2 actions to wrench it free by force, taking the core damaged (a lesser STC). That is the only difference the take makes — claiming the core, by either means, is the act break.
  3. Extract it: the Drudge picks up the Core token and bears it to the Surface Breach at normal speed; any other model carries it at half speed (no Drudge → a crawling fighting retreat).

Act 1 — Descent (stay silent)

The tomb is mostly dormant; presence wakes it. Track Noise (0–6, starts 0). It rises from:

  • +1 at the end of each round the force is in or past the machine-hall — your heat registers.
  • +1 in any round your force makes a ranged attack: gunfire rings through the vault. Melee and silent kills add nothing — blades, not bolters, on the way down.
  • +1 for each optional haul prised loose (see below; a failed ward adds another).

Noise is how loud the dark is — and the dark answers in kind. At the end of each round in the machine-hall or below, the tomb wakes a cohort from the surrounding black. It is never a lone straggler, and it is cumulative — the louder you have been, the deeper it stacks:

  • A drone pack — always. Roll 1d3 + current Noise servitor-drones.
  • + a construct at Noise 3+ — a gun- or blade-servitor joins the pack.
  • + a guardian at Noise 5+ — a guardian-class construct rises with them.

Stay quiet and you face a handful of drones to knife in the dark; get loud and every round throws a swelling pack and the heavier constructs and, past 5, a guardian — again and again, until the core is yours. Whatever wakes, you must answer it now, before the core is even yours — and whatever still stands when you claim it comes with you into the run.

Stay Quiet (Navy / Sisters). Each round the shield-teams move silent and keep the choke clear, the end-of-round Noise tick is negated — they mask the team's passage. A clean descent scores their spotlight Tac Op (XP).

All of Act 1 is one question: reach and claim the core with Noise as low as you can — because every point of it wakes bigger things around you now, and because whatever you have woken comes with you into the run. You are never taxed for taking the core; the take is the act break.

Act 2 — Extraction (the world wakes)

Claiming the core slams the tomb to full alarm — and from here the Noise track is done. Discard it. Two things cross the act break: the constructs already on the board (whatever Act 1 woke comes with you — a quiet descent arrives with the floor nearly clear; a loud, greedy one arrives already swarmed), and the core on the Drudge's back.

The tomb floods. At the end of every round, no matter what you do, the tomb throws its full cohort — a drone pack, the heavier constructs, and a guardian-class construct — the worst of Act 1 now guaranteed and relentless. They bear down the breach-path and accumulate: stand still and you are buried. This is the engine of Act 2 — not a clock you can pause, a tide that rises every round.

Snares are your only brake. The traps you laid in the quiet (up to 3) are spent here to pin, thin, or wall off the pursuit — a round bought each. A clean team, intact, traps set, the floor not already full of what they woke, can reach the breach ahead of the flood. A team that arrives late, hurt, trapless, with Act 1's guardians already on them, drowns — run out of snares before you run out of tomb and the cohorts take you.

The Seal (the deadline). A countdown of 6: +1 at the end of each round after the claim. At 6 the Surface Breach seals — only models (and the core) already out are saved; the rest are entombed alive. Between the rising tide and the closing door, there is only the run.

Snares & traps (this scenario only)

The forge-tomb is a killing-ground twice over: the ancients trapped it, and the Scavengers know how to trap it back. Both halves run only here.

The tomb bites the greedy. Every side-vault haul sits behind a ten-thousand-year-old counter-intrusion ward. Before an operative takes a haul they must pass a disarm test — the acting operative's relevant characteristic (GM's call: Reflexes, Tech, or the like):

  • Pass — the vault opens clean; take the haul (its usual +1 Salvage / +1 Suspicion, and +1 Noise in Act 1).
  • Fail — the ward fires: the operative takes a wound (or is knocked down), and in Act 1 the discharge is loud — +1 extra Noise. Greed is one risk; greed and clumsiness wakes the tomb.

The Scavengers trap it back (their spotlight). Rig-craft is the Forgers' trade. In Act 2, an enemy moving onto (or within 1" of) a Snare triggers it — and these traps are your one brake on the flood, each a single round bought against the rising cohorts. Carried in: the Suspicion track comes to a head here — every sin of the campaign is in the ledger now, and a fuller confession at the Oath (Ch.4) means you arrive with less of it: a longer leash before the Sisters lose faith. How cleanly they have carried themselves (Ch.5–6) decides whether the Sisters still believe in them when the secret finally breaks.

Briefing

Vigil Antias hangs in the dark like a forgotten lantern — a loyal Imperial station crewed by men and women who have done nothing wrong, except believe a lie about you. Inside its data-core sleeps the truth Drask buried. Sergeant Roe checks his weapon and, for once, does not look away. "We take the record. We do not paint these walls with the blood of the innocent. And then we do the one thing they will never expect of men like us." He sets the weapon down on the deck, and picks it up again. "We put our guns down, and we let the Emperor judge."

The board

An Imperial void-station interior: clean, lit, ordered — the opposite of every board before it. Sightlines that reward bypass and stealth, control nodes to seal sections, an Alarm response. The horror here is having to fight people who are right to oppose you.

Objectives

One continuous infiltration, Blackstone-style: get in unseen, reach the Data-Core Vault before Drask's Agent destroys the truth, put him down for it, and then surrender — weapons grounded, evidence in hand — to the Station Commander. You win by being cleared: the intact sealed logs, laid before the Commander, with the blood on your hands light enough that he believes them.

1. Infiltrate (stay unseen). The station is crewed by loyal Imperials who have done nothing wrong. Move by stealth from the Docking Ring inward, using cover and sightlines to slip patrols. Track Alarm (starts 0): being spotted — or a body found — raises it; at thresholds the station tightens, sealing sections and then loosing an Arbites response team. A maxed Alarm does not end the mission; it floods the board.

2. Silence only when forced (+Suspicion). A Penitent may make a silent take-down of a lone loyalist — but every one is +1 Suspicion and one more innocent on the ledger. A body left in the open risks discovery (Alarm). You can cut a path to the vault; you will arrive a murderer, and the Sisters will be counting.

3. Race the Agent (the burn clock). Drask's Agent, a sleeper already aboard, reached the vault ahead of you and is destroying the record. He has a two-round head start, and you cannot catch him in the corridors — he is always ahead; do not chase him, race the clock instead. He needs three rounds at the vault: he begins cleansing at the end of Round 2 and finishes at the end of Round 5. If no Penitent has engaged him by the end of Round 5, the record is ash (→ Hollow). The cruelty is the choice — creep, and arrive too late; or push hard, louder and bloodier, and reach him in time.

4. Defeat the Agent (the one fight you must win). He is a tough, many-wound killer — a boss, not a mook; expect him to take the team several activations to bring down. The instant a Penitent attacks him, the burn stops for good — he turns from the record to fight. From there it is a straight brawl: put him down and the sealed original is yours, intact.

5. Surrender, and be judged. Carry the recovered logs — and yourselves — to the Station Commander at the Command Spire. Ground your weapons. Sabine Orr vouches, officer to officer, and lays the authenticated record before him (no Orr → you surrender data you cannot prove you did not forge: a Hollow clearing). How he receives you is set by your run:

  • Clean (low Suspicion, few or no innocents dead) — the Agent's sabotage corroborates every word. Cleared and released. Full exoneration.
  • Bloody (high Suspicion, many dead, or the Sisters forewarned him) — the truth is undeniable, and so is the blood on his deck. Cleared in law, stained in fact: a Hollow clearing.
  • No proof (the record burned) — you have only your word, and your word is "deserter." There is nothing to surrender to; you run. Failed.

An official clearing is an excuse the whole Covenant can stand behind — the Imperium has vouched for the Navy now, and the desertion-heat the crew has carried all campaign falls away with it. Being cleared cuts Suspicion hard: to 0 on a clean run, and still sharply on a bloody one (the charge is legally answered even if the deck ran red). Only a Failed run leaves the secret — and the heat — intact.

The Sisters' doubt (Suspicion 4+). If Suspicion crosses 4 at any point, the Sisters lose faith and contact the Station Commander themselves: there are mutineers aboard. This does not end the mission — it can still be completed — but the station now believes the worst before you reach the Spire: more guardsmen deploy, the Alarm climbs faster, and the surrender turns hostile. Your campaign-long Suspicion comes due here.

The Opposition

  • Loyal Imperial Navy armsmen & Arbites — competent, lawful, and not evil. They patrol; they are not hunting you until you give them cause. Every one you kill is an innocent and +1 Suspicion. The Alarm track brings heavier waves — lockdowns, then an Arbites response team — and the Sisters' warning (Suspicion 4+) brings more of them, sooner.
  • Drask's Agent — the lone genuinely hostile actor aboard: a planted killer racing to burn the one record that damns his master. A tough, many-wound boss who cannot be caught before the vault — he must be beaten at it, and only by reaching him in time.
  • The Station Commander — not an enemy. The man you must convince by evidence, not force, that you were never the traitors. Spill enough blood reaching him and you prove the Navy right about you.

Twists & secrets

  • The surrender (the whole point). Deserters do not turn themselves in — these ones do. That is the redemption: not the theft of a truth and a clean getaway, but the nerve to ground your weapons before Imperial justice and trust it. A clean run makes that trust land; a bloody one makes a mockery of it.
  • The Sisters' verdict is your own ledger. They are not on the board, but they are reading the Suspicion track from afar. Cross 4 and they decide you are exactly what Drask said you were — and they tell the station so. The covenant's fate in Ch.8 is written by how cleanly you take this place.
  • The Agent's head start. He is meant to reach the vault first. The question was never "can we hunt him down quietly" — it is "how much will you spend, in noise and blood and the Sisters' faith, to reach him before the record dies."

The campaign's climax. Gnashrak's Maw lies wrecked inside the Blackstone Fortress (Ch.8), its warp-drive dead. The only thing that can revive it is an archeotech power core buried somewhere in the shifting dark. Everything the Covenant has become is spent here, together, one last time.

Briefing

The Blackstone Fortress does not have corridors so much as intentions. The walls are black glass that swallows light and gives back reflections that move when you don't. Geometry lies here; distance lies; the dark is full of patient, ancient things. Somewhere in this impossible maze sleeps the power to fly free — and the Fortress has kept it safe for longer than humanity has existed. Adept Vire's stolen Fragment grows warm against their chest, and pulses, as if it has finally come home. "It's showing me the way," Vire whispers. "Throne help us — it wants us to follow."

The board

The labyrinth: shifting black-glass corridors, vaults, a cathedral-vast central chamber (the Spindle), and the core chamber at its heart. Objectives

The mini-game — The Reckoning

A deterministic closing procedure. Resolve in order; nothing here is left to chance.

1. Read the threads (above), team by team. The GM narrates each fixed ending from the sheet.

2. Name the dead. Speak every lost character aloud. The Covenant's final dignity is that none are forgotten.

3. Score the Covenant Standing. Add it up:

ConditionPoints
Suspicion ended Low (covenant never broke)+2
Each clean chapter: Ch.5 Consecrated / Ch.7 Cleared / Ch.9 Together+1 each
All three role-leads (Pilot, Tech-Adept, Hospitaller) survived+1
Covenant pool was pledged generously at the Oath (no hoarders)+1
The Fragment was Surrendered (not Kept or Claimed)+1
Covenant ever formally broke / Suspicion hit max−2
Each role-lead permanently lost−1 each

4. The last choice (gated by Standing):

StandingOption unlockedParting
5 or moreTrue Covenant. The teams may choose to stay one warband — Gnashrak's Maw sails on united.Stay bound, or part as kin.
2 to 4Honoured Parting. They part as comrades; the door to reunion stays open.Part, with debts paid and respect kept.
1 or lessCold Parting. The bond was only ever a contract.Scatter — three separate threads into the dark.

5. The closing tone (optional flourish). Each surviving role-lead lays one token — who they became — into a pool. Majority colours the tone of the final beat only: mostly bright → triumphant; split → bittersweet; mostly dark → grim. Tone, never substance.

Outcomes

Record the final fates exactly as resolved — for memory, and for any sequel:

  • Penitents: Exonerated / Fugitive / Martyred.
  • Sisters: Consecrated / Marred, and Helewys's final regard.
  • Scavengers: Ascended / Changed / Damned / Empty-handed, and the Fragment's fate.
  • The Covenant Standing and the last choice (Bound / Parted-kin / Scattered).
  • The dead — the full roll.

Carry-forward (for a possible sequel)

  • True Covenant → Bound: Gnashrak's Maw sails on as a single united warband with a galaxy of unfinished business — Drask's surviving allies, the STC's true worth, and whatever the Fragment began at Blackstone. One campaign continues.
  • Honoured / Cold Parting: three threads spiral outward, any one a future campaign — the Sisters' next shrine, the Forgers' STC world, the Penitents' reckoning with the Navy that named them traitor.
